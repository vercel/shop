Something is happening to commerce, and something related but distinct is happening to the code that powers it. On the commerce side: agents are beginning to do what we spent a decade training customers to do themselves. They browse, they compare, they make decisions, they transact. The protocols for this are being written right now. Not metaphorically right now. Literally, as you read this, someone is merging a pull request that will matter. On the development side: the majority of code in new projects is getting generated before it is reviewed. These two shifts reward flexibility over architectures that assume the future will look just like the present.

The case for composable commerce has always been a case for freedom. Freedom to choose the best search provider, the best CMS, the best page URLs, and to not have those choices made for you. We know that for a long time, freedom came with real complexity, and a legitimate calculation that owning every seam in your stack meant owning every problem in it. That calculation has changed. The code has never been better, nor has the tooling to put that code together.

This template is what that proof looks like. Vercel and Shopify built it together, which is to say: two companies that each built the thing the other one needed decided to wire them together properly and ship it. Next.js, the AI SDK, a caching architecture that handles the hard performance problems before you have to think about them, and Shopify Storefront APIs ready to go the moment your credentials are. The interesting parts are waiting for you. The tedious parts are already done.

What you do with it from there is the point. Swap in your CMS. Connect your search provider. Integrate whatever you're convinced matters. We've been opinionated about the pieces that should be stable and open about the pieces that should move. Composable commerce used to require a certain kind of hard-won experience just to set up correctly. We think it should just work.
